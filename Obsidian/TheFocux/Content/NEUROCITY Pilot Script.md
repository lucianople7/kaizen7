# NEUROCITY Pilot

## Episode 1: The Notification Siege

Target length: 5 minutes.  
Learning objective: explain why notifications and task switching disrupt demanding work without reducing the explanation to “dopamine addiction”.

## Cold open — real world

Visual: a founder is writing a difficult proposal. A notification appears. They glance for two seconds, return to the document and stop.

Narrator:

> The interruption lasted two seconds. Getting the task back may take much longer.

Cut into NeuroCity.

## Scene 1 — Signal Exchange

Thousands of ordinary signals move through the station. A bright notification capsule arrives marked `URGENT`, even though its contents are trivial.

Dopi:

> New signal. Unknown value. Could be important.

Dopi reroutes it toward Executive Tower.

## Scene 2 — Executive Tower

FOCUX is coordinating a complex sequence across several panels. The notification forces every panel to pause.

FOCUX:

> Dopi, was that signal relevant to the active objective?

Dopi:

> I did not say relevant. I said new.

The original task remains visible, but its next step has disappeared.

## Scene 3 — Wrong explanation

Corti sees stalled panels and activates emergency protocol.

Corti:

> Productivity is falling. Increase pressure.

Timers appear everywhere. The city becomes faster and less coordinated.

FOCUX:

> Stand down. This is not an emergency. It is a routing problem.

## Scene 4 — Neuro reality check

Neuro freezes the city and addresses the audience.

Neuro:

> Dopamine is not a tiny villain addicted to notifications. It participates in motivation, learning, prediction and salience. The useful question is not “how do I remove dopamine?” It is “which signals am I training my system to treat as important?”

Evidence slate:

- Interruptions compete for limited control.
- Returning to a task requires reconstructing context.
- Novelty can be salient without being valuable.

Neuro:

> NeuroCity is a metaphor. Real attention depends on distributed networks, context, sleep, motivation and the task itself.

## Scene 5 — System response

FOCUX changes the routing rules:

1. Non-critical signals wait outside Executive Tower.
2. The active task shows one visible next action.
3. Dopi receives meaningful progress markers instead of random alerts.
4. Corti receives a scheduled all-clear signal.

Dopi:

> So I still get progress?

FOCUX:

> Progress, yes. Every vibration, no.

## Resolution — real world

The founder places the phone out of sight and writes a visible note: `Next: define the three decision criteria.` Work resumes.

Narrator:

> Focus is not the absence of every distraction. It is a system that knows what deserves priority.

## End card

> One experiment: remove visible notifications for one demanding work block and write the next physical action before starting.

CTA:

> Read the evidence dossier at THE FOCUX.

## Scientific review checklist

- Avoid a precise recovery-time statistic unless the underlying study and task match the claim.
- Do not describe dopamine as pleasure or addiction alone.
- Distinguish external interruption from mind-wandering.
- Do not imply that notification settings treat a clinical attention disorder.
- Add primary references before recording.
